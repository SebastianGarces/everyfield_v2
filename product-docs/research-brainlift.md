## 1. Current Tooling Landscape for Church Planters

### 1.1 The Fundamental Gap: No End-to-End Planting Process Software Exists

The church technology market is saturated with **Church Management Software (ChMS)** designed for *established* churches: Planning Center (45 of the 100 fastest-growing churches), Rock RMS (22), Tithely/Breeze, Realm, ChurchTrac, and others. These platforms handle post-launch operations: giving, check-ins, service planning, group management, and member databases.

**However, there is no comprehensive platform purpose-built for the church planting *process* itself** -- the 12-36 month journey from calling to launch day. The tools that exist fall into narrow categories:

**Source:** [ChMeetings - Software for Church Plants](https://www.chmeetings.com/blog/the-ideal-software-solution-for-growing-church-plants/) | [ChurchTechToday - 9 Technologies for Every Church Plant](https://churchtechtoday.com/9-important-technologies-every-church-plant/) | [Grain Blog - Best ChMS 2026](https://grainledger.com/blog/best-church-management-software) | [Church Management Software - Fastest Growing Churches 2025](https://www.churchmanagementsoftware.com/fastest%E2%80%91growing-churches-2025/)

### 1.2 What Does Exist (and Its Limitations)

#### PlanterPlan (Passion for Planting)
- **What it is:** A freemium task management tool specifically for church planting, organized into 6 phases broken into milestones and tasks
- **Features:** Checklist templates, built-in date engine for due dates, team collaboration, delegation, progress reports and graphs
- **Cost:** Free (Phase 1 included); $199 one-time upgrade per project for premium content across all 6 phases
- **Limitations:** Fundamentally a *task checklist*, not a platform. No CRM, no financial tracking, no document generation, no assessment tools, no network-level oversight. Designed by and for individual planters, not sending networks managing portfolios of plants.
- **Source:** [PlanterPlan](https://planterplan.com/) | [Passion for Planting - Church Planting Software](https://church-planting.net/church-planting-software/) | [Passion for Planting - 6 Steps of Planning](https://church-planting.net/six-steps-of-planning/)

#### PlanterApp
- **What it is:** An assessment and portfolio tool for church planting candidates
- **Features:** Aggregates assessment results into an online dashboard, generates application portfolios for job applications, direct submission to participating organizations
- **Limitations:** Assessment-only. No project management, no ongoing tracking, no operational support post-assessment.
- **Source:** [Passion for Planting - Church Planting Software](https://church-planting.net/church-planting-software/)

#### Generic Tools Cobbled Together
Church planters overwhelmingly rely on generic, disconnected tools:
- **Project management:** Monday.com, Asana, Trello (not church-specific)
- **Spreadsheets:** Excel/Google Sheets for budgets, contact tracking, fundraising progress, attendance
- **Communication:** Mailchimp, text messaging platforms
- **Design:** Canva for graphics, logos, social media
- **Website:** Squarespace, Nucleus
- **Giving:** Subsplash, Kindrid
- **CRM:** No standard -- personal spreadsheets, phone contacts, or generic CRMs

Churches have historically "muddled through" by "cobbling together church management software alongside emails, spreadsheets, and perhaps a Google Group or some other free software." This patchwork creates situations where "projects consistently cost more money, time, and materials than expected, leadership teams feel discouraged because projects aren't going as planned, staff or volunteers aren't clear on what needs to be done or who's responsible."

**Source:** [Church Production Magazine - Best Project Management Tools for Churches](https://www.churchproduction.com/magazine/best-project-management-tools-for-churches/) | [Churchlytics - Why Project Management Software](https://churchlytics.com/why-project-management-software/) | [Passion for Planting - Free Church Planting Downloads](https://church-planting.net/church-planting-downloads/download-tag/ms-excel-workbook/)

### 1.3 ChMS Platforms Marketing to Church Plants

Several established ChMS platforms market "church plant" tiers (Breeze offers a free first year; Subsplash has a church plants use case page; ChMeetings targets growing plants). But these are **church management tools with a discount**, not planting process tools. They help you manage what you've built, not build it.

**Source:** [Breeze ChMS for Church Plants](https://www.breezechms.com/lp/church-plant-offer) | [Subsplash - Church Plants](https://www.subsplash.com/use-cases/church-plants) | [Subsplash - 15 Church Planting Resources](https://www.subsplash.com/blog/15-church-planting-resources-for-success)

### 1.4 The Gap Summary

| Need | Current Solution | Gap |
|------|-----------------|-----|
| Phase-based task management | PlanterPlan (basic) | No CRM, finance, or network integration |
| Planter assessment | PlanterApp, network-specific tools | Siloed; no connection to ongoing journey |
| Relationship/People management | Spreadsheets, personal contacts | No church-planting-specific CRM |
| Financial tracking & fundraising | Excel, QuickBooks, Aplos | Not integrated into planting phases |
| Document generation | Word templates, PDFs | Manual; no templating tied to methodology |
| Network oversight of multiple plants | Salesforce (custom), spreadsheets | No purpose-built portfolio dashboard |
| Coaching/mentorship tracking | Phone calls, email, memory | Unstructured; no progress visibility |
| Integrated learn + plan + execute | **Nothing** | **This is the core whitespace** |

## 2. How Sending Networks Currently Operate

### 2.1 NAMB / Send Network (Southern Baptist Convention)

**Scale:** The largest Protestant church planting network in North America. In 2025, their orientation event set a record with 217 endorsed church planters.

**Process:**
1. **Interest Form:** Online submission to begin the pipeline
2. **Pre-Assessment:** Three online questionnaires covering church planting capacity, personal character, and marriage health
3. **Assessment Retreat:** Two-day event measuring readiness across 9 essential areas: calling, emotional and spiritual health, family dynamics, vision, leadership, communication, missional engagement, disciple-making, and social skills
4. **Multiplication Pipeline:** A 1-3 year development process with three competency-based levels:
   - **Level 1 (Living on Mission):** 8 competencies -- Spiritual Formation, Bible Fluency, Evangelism, Relationships, etc.
   - **Level 2 (Disciple Making):** 8 competencies -- Self-Awareness, Communication, Disciple Making, Church Planting, etc.
   - **Level 3 (Church Planting):** 7 competencies -- Healthy Emotions, Family Dynamics, Preaching, etc.
5. **Endorsement & Orientation:** Planters receive a five-year planter pathway with benefits and potential financial assistance

**Assessment Methods:** Self-awareness profiles with peer feedback, philosophy statements, personal action plans, fitness assessments, vision casting presentations, sermon manuscript development and evaluation, interviews with experienced leaders. Competency-based, emphasizing practical demonstration over written tests.

**Tracking:** They use a combination of web registration systems, online assessment tools, and a structured five-year pathway. No publicly identified proprietary software platform; their online pipeline tool is described as "simple to use" but not detailed publicly.

**Source:** [Send Network - Assessment](https://www.namb.net/send-network/church-planting/assessment/) | [Send Network - The Process](https://www.namb.net/send-network/pipeline/process/) | [Send Network - Multiplication Pipeline](https://www.namb.net/send-network/pipeline/) | [NAMB Helpdesk - Assessments](https://helpdesk.namb.net/support/solutions/articles/4000143942) | [Send Network - Benefits](https://www.namb.net/send-network/benefits/) | [Send Network - Orientation Record](https://www.namb.net/send-network/resource/send-network-orientation-2025/)

### 2.2 ARC (Association of Related Churches)

**Scale:** A global family of church and business leaders; one of the largest church planting networks.

**Process (4 Steps):**
1. **ARC Launch Training Event:** Recommended 12-18 months before launch day. Hear from ARC pastors, coaches, and staff on the "nuts and bolts of church planting."
2. **Application & Assessment:** Evaluates three areas: Marriage, Money, and Ministry. Includes online application, readiness assessment with feedback, and the "8 Essentials of Church Planting" video series with Pastor Chris Hodges.
3. **Online Training Platform & Coaching:** Access to "ARC All Access" -- an online training platform to build a launch plan. Includes training courses, a digital training manual, resource library, and resource partners. Planters are connected with a regional ARC Launch Coach (experienced planter now pastoring a thriving church). Monthly webinars and private coaching calls.
4. **Funding & Launch:** Dollar-for-dollar match of first $50,000 raised. Planter goes to new location, gathers team, runs promotional activities, holds preview services, launches typically within six months of arrival.

**What ARC All Access appears to be:** An LMS-style training platform with courses and resources. Not a project management or operational tool.

**Source:** [ARC - Get Started](https://www.arcchurches.com/launch/get-started/) | [ARC - Launch](https://www.arcchurches.com/launch/) | [ARC - Church Planting](https://www.arcchurches.com/equip/planting/) | [ARC - Year to Launch PDF](https://www.arcchurches.com/files/year-to-launch.pdf)

### 2.3 Acts 29

**Scale:** A global network of churches bound together by relationship, theology, and church planting.

**Process:**
1. **Initial Application:** Short online form to determine timing and fit
2. **Phone Interview:** Candidate articulates vision for church planting to an Acts 29 assessor
3. **Assessment Conference:** Two-day event with case study discussions, strategic planning presentations, and individual interviews. Assesses giftedness in formal and informal settings. Wives included.
4. **Network Support:** Church-to-church partnerships, regional cohorts, annual pastors and wives retreats

**Effectiveness claim:** 70% of planters who go through assessment plant a church with Acts 29, and 97% of those churches are "healthy and sustainable."

**Key insight from Bob Thune (Acts 29 leader):** The pipeline that fueled Acts 29's boom (2007-2012, 200% growth) no longer exists. Previously, candidates were experienced ministry professionals with 5-10 years of proven experience. Now, seminary graduates view church planting as a first career option, lacking patience to serve on staff before launching. "Ready-made leaders who are theologically mature, missionally fruitful, self-aware, and ministry-experienced are harder and harder to find." Developing seminary graduates now requires a *decade*, not two years.

**Source:** [Acts 29 - What's Involved](https://www.acts29.com/whats-involved/) | [Acts 29 - Assessment Conferences](https://www.acts29.com/assessment-conferences/) | [Acts 29 - Assessment Process Testimonials](https://www.acts29.com/testimonials-of-the-acts-29-assessment-process/) | [Acts 29 - Unrivaled Assessment Process](https://www.acts29.com/unrivaled-assessment-process/) | [BobThune.com - The Pipeline is Shrinking](https://www.bobthune.com/blog/2017/06/pipeline-shrinking)

### 2.4 Stadia Church Planting

**Scale:** Non-denominational; has helped start new churches on 5 continents since 2003.

**Process:**
1. **Discovery Center:** Two phases -- Exploration ("Am I called to start a church?") and Discernment ("Am I ready to start a church?")
2. **Startup Services:** Full access to a Startup Coach, essential training library, 1-on-1 coaching, cohort participation, document preparation, and project management assistance. Also provides bookkeeping, 501(c)(3) filing, articles of incorporation, and EIN establishment.
3. **Post-Launch Support:** Care, insight, and guidance for ongoing health
4. **Multiplication Lab:** Support for churches ready to plant daughter churches

**Distinctive:** Stadia provides the most operationally hands-on support (bookkeeping, legal filings, project management) of any major network. Their services are free to planters, funded by a partner network.

**Source:** [Stadia - Discovery](https://stadia.org/discovery/) | [Stadia - Services](https://stadia.org/services/) | [Stadia - Startup](https://stadia.org/startup/) | [Stadia - FAQ](https://stadia.org/faq/)

### 2.5 Exponential

**Scale:** Partners with 50+ church planting networks and denominations. Hosts the largest annual church multiplication gatherings (10,000+ leaders across 8+ event locations).

**Role:** Exponential functions as a *resource aggregator and convener* rather than a direct planting network. They provide:
- 100+ free eBooks
- Hundreds of hours of video/audio training
- Annual multiplication themes with integrated resources
- Learning Communities (peer-to-peer cohorts of 25 leaders, 1-year journey)
- PlanterMatch (helps planters find networks)
- PlanterJobs (church planting job board)

**Source:** [Exponential](https://exponential.org/) | [Exponential - About](https://exponential.net/about/) | [PlanterMatch - Networks](https://plantermatch.org/networks/)

### 2.6 Church Multiplication Network (CMN / Assemblies of God)

**Process:** CMN Launch training events connect planters to relationships and help them create community-changing plans. Provides training videos, coaching, friendship, and partnerships with organizations like StartCHURCH for legal compliance.

**Source:** [Church Multiplication Network](https://www.churchmultiplication.net/) | [CMN Launch](https://www.churchmultiplication.net/launch)

### 2.7 Cross-Network Pattern Analysis

| Network | Assessment | Training Platform | Ongoing Tracking | Operational Support | Portfolio View |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|------------------|--------------------|--------------| 
| NAMB/Send | Rigorous (9 areas, 2-day retreat) | Online pipeline tool | 5-year pathway | Limited (content-focused) | Unknown/internal |
| ARC | Marriage/Money/Ministry + video series | ARC All Access (LMS) | Regional coaches | $50K match funding | Unknown/internal |
| Acts 29 | Two-day conference + phone interview | Minimal (church-to-church) | Regional cohorts | Minimal | Unknown/internal |
| Stadia | Discovery Center (2 phases) | Training library | Coaching | Extensive (bookkeeping, legal, PM) | Unknown/internal |
| Exponential | N/A (aggregator) | Massive content library | Learning communities | N/A | N/A |

**Key finding:** Every network has assessment processes and training content, but **none appear to have a unified software platform that tracks planters from assessment through launch and beyond.** The tracking appears to happen through a combination of internal databases, coaching relationships, and manual processes. This is the operational gap EveryField could fill at the network level.

## 3. Technology Resistance in Faith-Based Organizations

### 3.1 Academic Research

#### Study: "When Churches Discovered the Digital Divide" (Campbell & Jordan, 2023)
Published in Brill's *Ecclesial Practices* (Volume 10, Issue 1), this study of focus groups involving **478 church leaders from Indiana** identified four specific challenges:
1. Lack of internet accessibility
2. Digital literacy deficits
3. Generational gaps in digital knowledge
4. **"Digital reluctance" or technological hesitancy** -- active pushback from congregations when leaders suggested digital solutions

A unique finding: beyond practical barriers, many churches experienced an *ideological* factor where technology adoption was resisted on principle, not just due to capability gaps.

**Source:** [Brill - Ecclesial Practices](https://brill.com/view/journals/ep/10/1/article-p36_004.xml?language=en) | [COVID Religion Research - Digital Divide](https://www.covidreligionresearch.org/how-the-digital-divide-digital-reluctance-impact-churches-during-the-covid-19-pandemic/)

#### Study: "Faithful Innovation" (MDPI Religions, 2025)
Examines how Christian churches negotiate institutional logics for AI value alignment. Highlights the tension between operational efficiency and theological identity.

**Source:** [MDPI - Faithful Innovation](https://www.mdpi.com/2077-1444/16/3/302)

#### Survey: Flourishing Congregations Institute (Acadia Divinity College)
Surveyed Canadian Christian leaders about technology trends' importance for the church by 2035. On a 7-point scale (7 = extremely important), technology-related trends scored *below* the average of 5.2:
- **Artificial Intelligence:** 4.42 (fourth lowest across all trends)
- Hybrid approaches, digitalization, and new forms of gathering all scored below average

The researchers concluded there is "a technology-adverse culture held by Christian leaders." They also noted the historical irony: the church embraced the printing press (enabling the Reformation) and pipe organs (cutting-edge technology in their era), yet modern technology acceleration generates resistance.

**Source:** [Flourishing Congregations Institute](https://flourishingcongregations.org/faith-and-the-future-navigating-the-churchs-reluctance-toward-emerging-technologies/)

#### COVID Pandemic Impact
The Tech in Churches During Covid-19 Project found that "of all the new skills and traits religious leaders had to take on during the pandemic, **technology innovation and decision-making was the most stressful and the one area for which they felt the most unprepared.**"

**Source:** [COVID Religion Research](https://www.covidreligionresearch.org/how-the-digital-divide-digital-reluctance-impact-churches-during-the-covid-19-pandemic/)

### 3.2 The "Faith vs. Data" Tension

This is a deeply felt tension in church contexts. Multiple sources articulate it:

**The Scriptural Objection:** "Paul told the church at Corinth to 'walk by faith, not by sight.' Is being informed by data the opposite of that?" Many pastors view data analysis as "antithetical to what Scripture teaches." The fear is that churches become "filled with numbers, instead of the Spirit."
-- Lifeway Research, "Hot Hands & Cold Churches: Christianity in the Age of Analytics"

**The Reconciliation Framework:** Researchers and practitioners propose being "data-informed, faith-driven" rather than "data-driven": "You know the importance of having data and using it for information, but not be driven by it. God's voice is always bigger than the data. With faith, God can do more than what we see in the data."
-- FaithTech Institute

**Biblical Counter-Arguments for Data:**
- Numbers 1 and 26: God commanded Moses to create demographic databases and assess military capacity
- Luke 14: Jesus invited people to "calculate the cost" before commitment
- Nehemiah: Gathered information about community conditions to enable spiritual action

**The Balance:** "Leaders must remain prepared to walk away from the rational to follow the Holy Spirit when necessary." Data serves as a "prompter" for awareness, not a replacement for discernment.

**Source:** [Lifeway Research - Hot Hands & Cold Churches](https://research.lifeway.com/2018/07/24/hot-hands-cold-churches/) | [FaithTech - Is God Against Data-Driven Church?](https://medium.com/faithtech/does-god-against-data-driven-church-97c5b3733adf) | [Christianity Today - From Guessing to Knowing](https://www.christianitytoday.com/partners/state-of-the-church/from-guessing-to-knowing-help-churches-flourish/)

### 3.3 Current State (2026)

Despite historical resistance, adoption is accelerating. A 2026 Barna/Pushpay report indicates church leaders are "moving beyond examining whether to adopt technology and instead asking what role it should play." However, only **33% of church leaders report their church uses AI**, while **64% say it's important to have an AI policy but only 5% actually have one**.

**Source:** [Subsplash - 100 Church Tech Trends 2026](https://www.subsplash.com/blog/100-must-know-facts-about-technology-church-trends) | [FaithTeams - AI in 2026](https://faithteams.com/ai-in-2026/)

### 3.4 Implications for EveryField

Technology resistance in church contexts is real but evolving. The product must be positioned as:
- A tool that *serves* the Spirit-led process, not one that replaces spiritual discernment
- "Data-informed, faith-driven" rather than "data-driven"
- A means of freeing planters to focus on people and relationships (reducing administrative burden)
- Complementary to mentorship, not a replacement for it

## 4. Counter-Arguments to Structured Methodology

### 4.1 The "One Size Doesn't Fit All" Argument

**Core criticism:** "One size doesn't fit all when it comes to church planting, and there are many different types of new churches that are contextualized to a variety of different settings and cultures."

Different churches have "different church cultures, backgrounds, communities, sizes, staff, and facilities, and merely copying what another church does may not be a good fit and therefore not be as effective."

There is also a theological dimension: 1 Corinthians 12:4-6 describes diverse spiritual gifts and service types, suggesting contextual approaches better align with biblical principles than standardization.

**Source:** [MinTools - Cookie Cutter Approaches Lessen Effectiveness](https://mintools.com/blog/cookie-cutter-approaches.htm) | [Asbury Center for Church Multiplication - Models of Church Planting](https://asburychurchplanting.com/models-of-church-planting/)

### 4.2 The Contextual Variation Problem

Responses to church planting challenges vary dramatically by context:
- **Central America:** Leaders feel the most urgent challenge is making disciples
- **Brazil:** Leaders say they must "return Christ to the center of the church"
- **Balkans:** Leaders identify allowing every Christian to take their rightful place in the priesthood of believers
- **Spain:** Leaders identify a lack of missional vision as their number one challenge

A standardized approach would address none of these correctly.

**Source:** [Lausanne Movement - 5 Truths for Church Planting Today](https://lausanne.org/global-analysis/5-truths-church-planting-today)

### 4.3 Speed vs. Depth Tension

Critics argue that systematized, rapid approaches sacrifice theological depth: "If we're being rapid about church planting, how do we avoid the unbiblical appointment of young believers to eldership?" God "usually does things slowly and missions is a slow and methodical process because it focuses on building relationships."

**Source:** [Lausanne - Church Planting Movements](https://lausanne.org/about/blog/church-planting-movements-why-is-the-worlds-most-proven-church-planting-method-still-controversial) | [House Church Me - Planting Movements Critique](https://housechurch.me/planting-movements-critique/)

### 4.4 The Six Dangers (Desiring God / Mack Stiles)

A widely-cited article identifies six dangers of church planting as a movement:
1. Sloppy definitions of what constitutes a "church"
2. Vulnerability to error and heresy
3. Temptations to pragmatism over theological fidelity
4. Lack of clarity on conversion
5. Ethnically homogeneous congregations
6. Over-contextualization -- changing the gospel message to fit culture

**Source:** [Desiring God - What Could Be Wrong with Church Planting?](https://www.desiringgod.org/articles/what-could-be-wrong-with-church-planting)

### 4.5 Assessment Tool Limitations

"There are no cookie-cutter answers to starting a church plant, and there is not one template or protocol that guides church planting." Simple assessment instruments "are not intended to replace more elaborate and expensive instruments." There are "no magical assessments that automatically guarantee success."

**Source:** [UMC Discipleship - Assessment Tools for Discerning a Call to Plant](https://www.umcdiscipleship.org/articles/assessment-tools-for-discerning-a-call-to-plant) | [Church Planting Catalyst - Assessment Methods](https://www.churchplantingcatalyst.com/assessment-methods)

### 4.6 How EveryField Should Address These Counter-Arguments

These criticisms are legitimate and EveryField must not ignore them. The platform should:
- Offer **configurable methodology** (not a rigid single playbook) -- support multiple church planting models and denominational contexts
- Position itself as providing *structure*, not *prescription* -- guardrails, not rails
- Allow networks and coaches to customize phases, milestones, and tasks for their specific context
- Never claim to replace Spirit-led discernment or relational mentorship
- Frame assessments as developmental tools, not gatekeeping mechanisms

## 5. Knowledge Codification in Church Planting

### 5.1 The Knowledge is Overwhelmingly Tacit

Church planting knowledge exists primarily in the heads of experienced practitioners and is transferred through mentorship relationships, not documentation.

**Evidence:**
- "Mentorship is not solely about imparting knowledge but about sharing wisdom gleaned from lived experiences, involving listening attentively, asking good and probing questions, and offering guidance rooted in personal insight and wisdom."
- Formal church planter apprenticeships are typically 2-3 years under the mentorship of experienced church planters, explicitly because the knowledge is experiential.
- The best candidates historically had "7-10 years of ministry experience" -- knowledge gained through immersion, not study.

**Source:** [Ministry Magazine - Mentoring: A Way of Life](https://www.ministrymagazine.org/archive/2017/03/Mentoring-A-way-of-life) | [Baptist Press - Essential Role of Mentorship](https://www.baptistpress.com/resource-library/bptoolbox/bridging-generations-the-essential-role-of-mentorship-in-the-church/) | [Enrichment Journal - Equipping Church Planters for Success](https://enrichmentjournal.ag.org/Issues/2009/Fall-2009/Equipping-Church-Planters-for-Success)

### 5.2 This is a Known and Documented Problem

#### Research Gap Identified
Farah (2021) identified six gaps in church planting movements research, including explicitly: **"Documenting Movements with Respect to Verification, Metrics, and Administration"** and **"Identifying Best Practices and Effective Movement Strategies."** The absence of documentation is formally recognized as a research gap in the academic literature.

**Source:** [Asbury Seminary - Identifying Current Gaps in Church Planting Movements Research](https://place.asburyseminary.edu/gcrj/vol13/iss2/2/)

#### The Pipeline Crisis Makes It Worse
Bob Thune's analysis reveals the direct consequence of tacit-only knowledge: when the generation of experienced practitioners who mentored the 2007-2012 boom moves on, the pipeline shrinks. "Developing seminary graduates requires a decade, not two years" -- largely because the knowledge can only be transferred through prolonged proximity, not through any documented system.

**Source:** [BobThune.com - The Pipeline is Shrinking](https://www.bobthune.com/blog/2017/06/pipeline-shrinking)

#### Knowledge Sharing Research in Christian Communities
Research on knowledge sharing in Christian communities confirms: "Systematic documentation of projects is an often overlooked but extremely valuable mechanism... over time, these reports become a collective memory, preventing the repetition of mistakes and inspiring new initiatives." Organizations that succeed in converting tacit knowledge (personal experience difficult to put into words) into explicit knowledge (methods, guidelines, best practices) "become more resilient in the face of change."

The biblical mandate for knowledge transfer is explicit: Paul instructed Timothy, "And the things you have heard me say in the presence of many witnesses entrust to reliable people who will also be qualified to teach others" (2 Timothy 2:2). Teaching "loses its meaning if it remains with the person who receives it because it is intended to circulate and bear fruit in others."

**Source:** [ST Network - Knowledge Sharing in Christian Communities](https://st.network/analysis/top/knowledge-sharing-in-christian-communities.html)

### 5.3 The Failure Rate Connection

Church plant failure rates range from 30-70% depending on the study. A NAMB study shows survival rates declining over time: 99% survive Year 1, 92% Year 2, 81% Year 3, 68% Year 4.

**But the data strongly supports that training and structure improve outcomes:**
- When planters provide leadership training to church members, survivability increases by **more than 250%** (79% survived vs. 59% without)
- The Evangelical Covenant Church reports an **86% four-year survival rate** for "well-conceived projects"
- The Asbury Seminary meta-study (reviewing 54 doctoral dissertations, 41 journal articles, and 100+ books) concludes that "the assessment, preparation, and coaching processes for the pastoral leader have a dramatic impact" on church plant success

The implication: codified knowledge and structured processes demonstrably improve outcomes, but the field has not yet built the infrastructure to deliver them at scale.

**Source:** [Malphurs Group - 10 Deadly Church Planting Mistakes](https://malphursgroup.com/10-deadly-church-planting-mistakes/) | [Movements.net - How Many Church Plants Fail?](https://www.movements.net/blog/tag/How+many+church+plants+fail%3F) | [Outreach Magazine - Church Plant: Survive, Succeed, Thrive](https://outreachmagazine.com/features/10866-church-plant-survive-succeed-thrive.html) | [Asbury Seminary - State of Church Planting](https://place.asburyseminary.edu/jascg/vol19/iss2/2/) | [Enrichment Journal - Equipping Church Planters](https://enrichmentjournal.ag.org/Issues/2009/Fall-2009/Equipping-Church-Planters-for-Success)

### 5.4 Implications for EveryField

EveryField has the potential to be the infrastructure that **converts tacit church planting knowledge into explicit, reusable, improvable methodology** without losing the relational dimension. Specifically:
- Capture best practices from experienced planters and coaches as configurable templates
- Create a feedback loop where outcomes data improves the methodology over time
- Enable mentorship to be *augmented* by structure (coach sees planter's dashboard, not replaced by it)
- Reduce the "decade-long development" timeline by providing structured, guided pathways alongside mentorship

## 6. NewChurches.com and Similar Resource Sites

### 6.1 NewChurches.com (Send Network / NAMB)

**Overview:** Free resource site with 801+ resources. All content is free. Includes articles, courses, eBooks, podcast episodes, and "ready-to-use" resources.

**Content Organized Around:**
- **9 Topic Areas:** Character, Calling, Prayer, Mission, Theology, Teaching, The Church, The Nations, Practical Leadership
- **3 Planter Phases:** Pre-Launch, Growing (0-5 years), Multiplying

**Content Balance Assessment:**

The site **heavily skews toward pastoral formation and theology rather than planting mechanics:**

| Category | Example Content | Orientation |
|----------|----------------|-------------|
| Theology | "Theology Masterclass" (16 hours on God, salvation, Scripture, church mission) | Pastoral |
| Preaching | "How Do I Preach?" with Tony Merida and Matt Smethurst | Pastoral |
| Discipleship | "4 Steps of a Discipleship Pathway" | Pastoral |
| Character | Spurgeon's leadership axioms, spiritual formation | Pastoral |
| Resilience | Resources on discouragement and calling clarity | Pastoral |
| Systems | "How Do I Build Systems?" (podcast episode) | Operational |
| Staffing | "5 Principles for Hiring Staff" | Operational |
| Fundraising | "How Do I Raise Money?" (podcast episode) | Operational |

**Key finding:** The operational/mechanics content exists but is a **small fraction** of the total. There is no step-by-step operational guide for incorporation, budgeting timelines, facility acquisition checklists, team building workflows, or launch logistics. The site's strength is developing the *person* of the planter; its weakness is the *process* of the plant.

## 7. Synthesis: The Strategic Opportunity

### 7.1 The Whitespace

1. **No integrated platform exists** that covers the full church planting journey from assessment to launch to post-launch health
2. **Networks track planters manually** through coaching calls, spreadsheets, and internal databases -- not through purpose-built software
3. **Content is abundant but operational tooling is scarce** -- planters can learn *about* church planting but have no unified system to *do* it
4. **Knowledge remains tacit** and locked in mentorship relationships, creating a pipeline bottleneck as experienced leaders age out
5. **Failure rates are high** but demonstrably improve with structured processes -- the infrastructure to deliver structure at scale doesn't exist

### 7.2 Critical Success Factors for EveryField

Based on this research, the platform must:

1. **Lead with humility, not hubris.** Position as a servant to the planting process, not a replacement for spiritual discernment. Use "data-informed, faith-driven" language.
2. **Be configurable, not prescriptive.** Support multiple methodologies, denominations, and cultural contexts. Allow networks to customize.
3. **Augment mentorship, not replace it.** Give coaches visibility into planter progress; don't claim software can substitute for relational wisdom.
4. **Serve both planters AND networks.** The network-level portfolio view (tracking dozens or hundreds of plants) is a potentially more compelling initial sale than individual planter tools.
5. **Bridge content and execution.** The market has content (NewChurches, Exponential, ARC All Access). The gap is turning that content into actionable, trackable workflows.
6. **Respect the "faith vs. data" tension.** Frame metrics as stewardship tools, not performance metrics. Use language the audience trusts.
7. **Solve the knowledge codification problem.** Enable experienced practitioners to encode their methodology as reusable templates -- making tacit knowledge explicit without losing its relational context.

### 7.3 Competitive Moat Potential

PlanterPlan is the closest existing competitor, but it is:
- A task checklist, not a platform
- Individual planter-focused, with no network/sending church oversight
- No CRM, financial tracking, or document generation
- No assessment integration
- No coaching/mentorship tooling
- Static content, not a living methodology that improves with data

An integrated platform that combines all these dimensions -- and serves at the network level -- would have no direct competitor in the market.
