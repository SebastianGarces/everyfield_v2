# BrainLift: Structured Support, Knowledge Codification, and Data-Driven Improvement in Church Planting

## Owners

Sebastian Garces

## Purpose

The purpose of this BrainLift is to explore whether church planting, one of the most complex organizational launches in the faith-based world, suffers from a knowledge infrastructure problem that technology can meaningfully address. It examines whether the scattered, uncodified, and unmeasured nature of church planting methodology is a root cause of preventable failure, and whether a unified platform that combines guided execution, knowledge codification, and industry-wide analytics represents a defensible and impactful intervention.

This BrainLift draws on three categories of evidence: (1) church planting-specific data from Lifeway Research, NAMB, Barna, and major sending networks; (2) academic research on franchise replication, startup accelerators, knowledge codification, and collective impact; and (3) platform strategy research on tool-to-network evolution and data-driven improvement in social sector organizations.

### In Scope

- Church planting as a structured, multi-phase organizational launch
- The current state of tooling, methodology, and measurement in the church planting industry
- What predicts church plant success and failure, based on available data
- Adjacent domain evidence: franchise playbooks, startup accelerators, nonprofit scaling, knowledge codification
- The tension between structured methodology and the relational, contextual, Spirit-led nature of church planting
- Platform strategy: tool-first approaches, data flywheels, and the path from single-player tool to industry data backbone
- Counter-arguments and boundary conditions that could undermine the thesis

### Out of Scope

- Church management software for established churches (Planning Center, Breeze, etc.)
- General arguments for or against technology in worship or congregational life
- Denominational theology or ecclesiology debates
- Post-launch church operations beyond the planting journey (Phase 0 through self-sustainability)
- Specific product features or technical architecture of any particular platform

## DOK 4, Spiky Points of View (SPOVs)

### SPOV 1: Church planting failure is not primarily a faith problem, it is a knowledge infrastructure problem, and the evidence for this is substantial.

**Elaboration:** The church planting industry operates on an implicit assumption that outcomes are largely a function of calling, character, and divine provision. While these matter deeply, the data tells a different story about what separates surviving plants from failed ones. Unsupported church plants survive at roughly 68% after four years (NAMB 2007 study). But when planters receive structured assessment, coaching, and training, survival rates rise to 86% (Evangelical Covenant Church) and 90% (ARC) at four to five years. Assessed planters outperform non-assessed ones by over 20% in attendance and produce more than double the annual conversions by year four (Lifeway Research). Leadership training alone increases survivability by more than 250% (NAMB). These are not marginal differences. They suggest that a significant portion of church plant failure is preventable through structured support, and that the industry's current infrastructure for delivering that support is inadequate. Content is abundant (801+ free resources on NewChurches.com, 100+ free eBooks from Exponential), but operational tooling that turns content into guided, trackable execution does not exist.

### SPOV 2: The church planting industry has a knowledge codification crisis that is formally recognized in academic literature, and it is getting worse as experienced practitioners age out.

**Elaboration:** Church planting knowledge is overwhelmingly tacit, it lives in the heads of experienced practitioners and is transferred through years-long mentorship relationships, not through documentation or systems. Farah (2021), in a study published through Asbury Theological Seminary, formally identified "Documenting Movements with Respect to Verification, Metrics, and Administration" as a gap in church planting research. Bob Thune's pipeline analysis shows the direct consequence: the generation of experienced practitioners who mentored the 2007-2012 church planting boom is aging out, and developing their replacements now takes a decade rather than two years, precisely because the knowledge can only be transferred through prolonged personal proximity. The median planter age has risen from 36 to 42. The youth pastor pipeline, historically the primary feeder of new planters, has dried up. NAMB literally has "more money than men." This is not a recruitment problem. It is a knowledge infrastructure problem. The field has no scalable mechanism for converting what experienced planters know into something that can be taught, tracked, and improved without requiring a decade of personal apprenticeship.

### SPOV 3: The most transformative opportunity in church planting technology is not the planter-facing tool, it is becoming the data backbone of an industry that currently has no shared measurement system and no way to systematically learn what works.

**Elaboration:** Every major sending network, NAMB, ARC, Acts 29, Stadia, Exponential, CMN, invests heavily in content, assessment, and coaching. None have a unified software platform that tracks planters from assessment through launch and beyond. Network-level portfolio tracking happens through coaching calls, internal databases, and manual processes. There is no shared measurement system, no cross-network benchmarking, and no mechanism for the industry to aggregate outcomes data and learn from it. This is what Kania and Kramer (2011) call the missing "backbone organization" in their Collective Impact framework, the dedicated infrastructure that enables shared measurement and coordinated action. It is also what the social sector literature calls the "Indicator Trap": organizations that track activities (services held, attendees counted) with precision while outcome indicators remain absent, vague, or disconnected from program data. A platform that solves the planter's day-to-day problem (guided execution, CRM, financial tracking) generates data as a byproduct. That data, aggregated across hundreds or thousands of church plants, becomes the industry's first real evidence base for what actually predicts success. This follows the "come for the tool, stay for the network" pattern identified by Chris Dixon and empirically validated by Eli Chait's analysis of the 100 largest marketplaces, where single-player-mode platforms achieved 10x greater capital efficiency than other seeding strategies.

## DOK 3, Insights

### Insight 1: Structured support produces a measurable and dramatic improvement in church plant survival, from roughly 68% to 86-90%, yet the infrastructure to deliver that support at scale does not exist.

The data consistently shows that assessment, coaching, training, and structured methodology improve outcomes. But every major network delivers these through manual, labor-intensive processes (coaching calls, assessment retreats, spreadsheets). No unified platform connects assessment to ongoing execution to outcome measurement. The gap is not in knowing that structure helps, it is in the absence of scalable infrastructure to provide it.

### Insight 2: The church planting industry has abundant content but almost no operational tooling, and existing resources overwhelmingly focus on pastoral formation rather than the mechanics of planting.

NewChurches.com (Send Network's resource site) has 801+ resources, but roughly 80% focus on pastoral formation and theology while only 20% address operational mechanics. ARC All Access is an LMS, not an operational tool. Exponential is a content aggregator. PlanterPlan, the closest thing to process tooling, is a task checklist with no CRM, no financial tracking, no network oversight, and no assessment integration. Church planters cobble together spreadsheets, generic project management tools, and disconnected services. There is no integrated "learn + plan + execute + measure" platform.

### Insight 3: The knowledge codification problem in church planting is not theoretical, it is formally documented in academic literature and is structurally worsening.

Farah (2021) identified documentation and metrics as a formal research gap. Bob Thune's analysis shows the pipeline is shrinking because knowledge transfer requires a decade of personal apprenticeship that fewer young leaders are willing or able to undertake. The SECI model (Nonaka and Takeuchi) provides a validated framework for understanding this problem: the field is stuck in "Socialization" (tacit to tacit, through mentorship) and has not built the infrastructure for "Externalization" (tacit to explicit, through codification) or "Combination" (explicit to explicit, through systematic integration).

### Insight 4: Adjacent domain research strongly supports the thesis that structured, playbook-driven replication improves outcomes for complex organizational launches, and that the benefit is concentrated in the early phases when practitioners need it most.

Winter and Szulanski's study of nearly 2,500 franchise outlets showed that inaccurate replication of a proven template increases failure risk. Franchise survival advantages over independent businesses are concentrated in the first one to two years and then disappear, suggesting that the playbook's value is highest during launch, precisely when church planters are most vulnerable. A Wharton study of 8,580 startups across 408 accelerators found that structured programs improve outcomes by 3.4% in fundraising likelihood and $1.8M in capital raised, with first-time founders and those lacking formal training benefiting the most. A 2025 meta-analysis of 21 studies confirmed a statistically significant positive effect of accelerator participation on venture outcomes.

### Insight 5: Technology resistance in faith-based organizations is real and partially ideological, but it has a tested reconciliation framework, and the field is already moving past the question of whether to adopt technology toward how.

Campbell and Jordan (2023) documented "digital reluctance" as an ideological factor among 478 church leaders. The Flourishing Congregations Institute found AI scored only 4.42 out of 7 in importance among Christian leaders. The scriptural objection, "walk by faith, not by sight", is genuine. However, practitioners and researchers have developed the "data-informed, faith-driven" reconciliation framework: data serves as a prompter for awareness, not a replacement for discernment. Biblical precedent exists (Numbers 1 and 26, Luke 14, Nehemiah). A 2026 Barna/Pushpay report indicates church leaders are "moving beyond examining whether to adopt technology and instead asking what role it should play." The resistance is real but declining, and the framing matters more than the technology itself.

### Insight 6: The "come for the tool, stay for the network" platform strategy is empirically the most capital-efficient way to build a two-sided marketplace, and it maps precisely to the church planting use case.

Eli Chait's analysis of the 100 largest marketplaces found that single-player-mode platforms (starting as a standalone tool, evolving into a network) achieved 10x greater capital efficiency than other seeding strategies. OpenTable, Square, and Amazon all followed this pattern. In the church planting context, the planter-facing tool (guided execution, CRM, financial tracking) provides day-one value without requiring network effects. As adoption grows, the aggregated data becomes valuable to sending organizations (portfolio analytics, benchmarking, early warning systems). As sending organizations require their planters to use the platform, adoption accelerates. This creates the flywheel that HubSpot demonstrated in the CRM market, and that no one has yet built for church planting.

### Insight 7: The pipeline crisis, the number one concern across church planting networks, is a systems problem masquerading as a people problem, and a platform is uniquely positioned to address it.

Every major network reports that finding qualified planters is their top challenge. NAMB has "more money than men." The median planter age has risen from 36 to 42. The youth pastor pipeline has dried up. But the proposed solutions, "just ask" more people, extend preparation periods, build internal pipelines, all require better infrastructure for identifying, assessing, developing, and tracking potential planters over time. A platform that makes the planter development journey visible, measurable, and manageable transforms pipeline development from a relational guessing game into a structured, data-informed process, without removing the relational dimension.

### Insight 8 (Contrary): Standardized methodology has real limitations across diverse cultural, denominational, and contextual settings, and a platform that ignores this will fail regardless of how good the technology is.

This is the strongest counter-argument and it must be taken seriously. The Lausanne Movement documents that church planting challenges vary dramatically by context, from discipleship in Central America to missional vision in Spain. Mack Stiles identifies six dangers of church planting movements, including over-contextualization and pragmatism over theological fidelity. Research on franchise standardization shows an inverted U-shape: too little or too much standardization both reduce performance (Kaufmann and Eroglu, 1999). The "minimum critical specification" principle from Dees and Anderson (2004), codify only the fewest elements that produce impact while allowing local adaptation, is the design constraint. A platform must be configurable, not prescriptive. It must provide structure without imposing a single methodology. It must serve as guardrails, not rails.

### Insight 9 (Contrary): The church planting market is smaller and more fragmented than typical venture-scale opportunities, and the buyer (sending networks) may be slow to adopt and price-sensitive.

Approximately 4,000 churches are planted per year in the US across 45-60+ networks with different theologies, methodologies, and cultures. This is not a venture-scale consumer market. Church planters are under severe financial stress, 60% live below the national average income, 69% are bivocational in the first two years, and 33% have considered quitting over finances. Sending networks, while better funded (ARC gives $100K per plant, NAMB has $70M+ annually), have no precedent for purchasing planter-facing software platforms. The go-to-market must account for a fragmented buyer landscape, price sensitivity at the planter level, and the need to demonstrate ROI to network-level decision makers who may not be technology-forward.

#### Subcategory 1.2: Survival and Failure Rates

**Source:** NAMB 2007 Study; Lifeway Research; Evangelical Covenant Church; ARC

- **DOK 1:** The commonly cited "80% failure rate" has no known original source and is widely regarded as a myth. Actual data shows year-over-year survival rates of 99% (Year 1), 92% (Year 2), 81% (Year 3), and 68% (Year 4) for general church plants. Well-supported plants show significantly higher survival: 86% at four years (Evangelical Covenant Church) and 90% at five years (ARC). The 30-70% failure range reported across studies depends heavily on methodology, timeframe, and definition of failure.
- **DOK 2:** The gap between unsupported (~~68%) and supported (~~86-90%) survival rates is the single most important data point for the thesis. It demonstrates that failure is not random or primarily spiritual, a significant portion is preventable through structured support. This gap is the core value proposition for any platform that delivers assessment, coaching, and guided execution at scale.

#### Subcategory 1.3: What Predicts Success

**Source:** Ridley (Indiana University / Fuller Seminary); Lifeway Research; Christianity Today

- **DOK 1:** Dr. Charles Ridley identified 13 behavioral competencies of effective church planters, with the first six considered "knock-out factors" that cannot be taught. A 2003 study found assessed planters outperformed non-assessed ones by at least 20% in attendance each year during the first four years, with assessed planters averaging 25 annual conversions vs. 12 for non-assessed. Lifeway Research found that meeting location (schools outperform), adequate planter compensation, sponsoring church support, full-time status at launch, and higher startup funding all correlate with success. The best predictor of future success is past experiences, practices, and habits, not stated beliefs or future plans.
- **DOK 2:** The success predictors are measurable, trackable, and improvable through structured support, they are not purely spiritual or intangible. A platform that tracks these indicators can provide early warning systems for at-risk plants and evidence-based guidance for resource allocation. The finding that past behavior predicts future success better than stated intentions also supports the case for ongoing progress tracking rather than one-time assessment.

#### Subcategory 1.4: The Pipeline Crisis

**Source:** Send Institute; Bob Thune; Lifeway Research; Exponential

- **DOK 1:** The pipeline of potential church planters is the number one concern across networks. NAMB has more financial resources than qualified planters to fund. The median planter age has risen from 36 (2007) to 42 (current). The youth pastor pipeline, historically the primary feeder, has dried up. Gen Z is reluctant to enter low-paying church planting roles. Only 4-7% of existing US churches are reproducing (Exponential), meaning 93-96% generate no new planters. Developing a seminary graduate into a ready planter now takes approximately 10 years vs. 2 years for the experienced ministry professionals who fueled earlier booms.
- **DOK 2:** The pipeline crisis is structural, not cyclical. It stems from the industry's dependence on tacit knowledge transfer through prolonged personal mentorship, a model that does not scale when the pool of experienced mentors shrinks. A platform that codifies planter development milestones, makes the journey visible and trackable, and reduces the mentorship burden on individual coaches could structurally address the pipeline bottleneck.

A platform that integrates financial tracking into the planting journey, with visibility for sending organizations, could reduce the financial stress that drives a third of planters to consider quitting.

This is consistent with the broader pattern: the church technology market has focused on established churches because that is where the money is, leaving the planting process, a smaller but underserved market, without purpose-built tooling.

### Category 3: Adjacent Domain Evidence

#### Subcategory 3.1: Franchise Replication, Structure Reduces Failure

**Source:** Winter & Szulanski, "Replication as Strategy" (Organization Science, 2001); Winter, Szulanski, Ringov & Jensen, "Reproducing Knowledge" (Organization Science, 2011); Small Business Economics

- **DOK 1:** Winter and Szulanski's study of nearly 2,500 franchise outlets found that inaccurate replication and premature adaptation of a proven template increase the risk of franchise unit failure and stall network growth. The franchise survival advantage over independent businesses is approximately 6-8 percentage points in the first one to two years, but conditional on surviving that period, the difference disappears. Chick-fil-A's model, 0.3% acceptance rate, intensive training, retained ownership, standardization with local flexibility, produces exceptional consistency.
- **DOK 2:** The franchise analogy is powerful because it directly addresses the standardization vs. adaptation tension. The data shows that early-phase deviation from a proven template is dangerous because practitioners do not yet understand the causal relationships between specific practices and outcomes (what Winter and Szulanski call "causal ambiguity"). The playbook's value is highest when the practitioner is most vulnerable, exactly the church planting use case. The Chick-fil-A model further shows that extreme selectivity (assessment) combined with intensive support and standardization with local flexibility can produce outcomes far above industry averages.
- **Links:** [Winter & Szulanski (2001)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/10.1287/orsc.12.6.730.10084) | [Winter et al. (2011)](https://pubsonline.informs.org/doi/abs/10.1287/orsc.1110.0663) | [Kaufmann & Eroglu (1999)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0883902697000979)

#### Subcategory 3.2: Startup Accelerators, Structured Programs Improve First-Timer Outcomes

**Source:** Assenova & Amit (Wharton, Strategic Management Journal); Meta-analysis (Journal of Technology Transfer, 2025); Cohen, Fehder, Hochberg & Murray (Research Policy, 2019)

- **DOK 1:** A Wharton study of 8,580 startups across 408 accelerators in 176 countries found that accelerated startups were 3.4% more likely to raise venture capital, raised $1.8 million more in the first post-program year, and generated higher revenue. Programs incorporating structured learning sessions showed improved success rates. First-time founders and those lacking formal business education benefited most. A 2025 meta-analysis of 21 studies and 68 effect sizes confirmed a statistically significant positive effect of accelerator participation. Y Combinator achieves an 87% portfolio survival rate.
- **DOK 2:** The accelerator model, fixed-term, cohort-based, with structured curriculum, milestone checkpoints, peer accountability, and mentor oversight, maps directly to church planting. The finding that first-time founders without formal training benefit most is directly relevant: first-time planters without operational experience are precisely the users who would gain the most from a structured, guided platform. The cohort dimension also matters: planters going through the journey simultaneously can learn from each other, creating peer accountability that augments (not replaces) the coach relationship.
- **Links:** [Wharton - Do Accelerators Improve Startup Success Rates?](https://knowledge.wharton.upenn.edu/article/do-accelerators-improve-startup-success-rates/) | [Springer - Meta-analysis of Startup Accelerators (2025)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10961-025-10218-6) | [Cohen et al. (2019)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0048733319300939)

#### Subcategory 3.3: Collective Impact, Backbone Organizations Enable Shared Measurement

**Source:** Kania & Kramer, "Collective Impact" (Stanford Social Innovation Review, 2011); Dees, Anderson & Wei-Skillern, "Scaling Social Impact" (SSIR, 2004)

- **DOK 1:** The Collective Impact framework identifies five conditions for success: common agenda, shared measurement systems, mutually reinforcing activities, continuous communication, and a backbone support organization. The Strive initiative in Cincinnati engaged 300+ community leaders and saw 34 of 53 tracked success indicators trend positive within four years. Dees and Anderson introduced the "minimum critical specification" principle: define the fewest elements possible to produce the desired impact while allowing local adaptation.
- **DOK 2:** Sending networks are collective impact organizations, they coordinate many independent church plants toward shared goals. But they lack the backbone infrastructure that the framework says is essential, particularly shared measurement systems. A platform that provides the measurement layer across a network's portfolio enables the pattern that Strive demonstrated: when you measure, share, and coordinate around shared indicators, outcomes improve across the entire system. The "minimum critical specification" principle is also the design philosophy the platform must follow, codify only what matters, leave the rest flexible.
- **Links:** [Kania & Kramer (2011)](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/collective_impact) | [Dees et al. (2004)](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/scaling_social_impact)

#### Subcategory 3.4: Knowledge Codification, The SECI Model and Deep Smarts

**Source:** Nonaka & Takeuchi, SECI model; Baldissone et al. (Frontiers in Psychology, 2019); Leonard & Swap, "Deep Smarts" (Harvard Business Press, 2005); Dagenais et al. (Evaluation and Program Planning, 2020)

- **DOK 1:** The SECI model describes four modes of knowledge conversion: Socialization (tacit to tacit), Externalization (tacit to explicit), Combination (explicit to explicit), and Internalization (explicit to tacit). It was empirically validated in a 2019 study (n=838). Leonard and Swap's "Deep Smarts" research establishes that expert practitioner knowledge requires guided experience, stories-with-a-moral, and guided observation to transfer. A 2020 study on codifying community practitioner knowledge found that practitioners viewed the resulting codification document as "a useful and necessary tool to guide practices."
- **DOK 2:** Church planting expertise is the definition of "deep smarts", built through years of lived experience, highly contextual, and traditionally transferred only through personal mentorship. The SECI model reveals that the field is stuck in Socialization (mentor to mentee) without infrastructure for Externalization (encoding what experts know into reusable formats). A platform that helps experienced practitioners articulate and encode their methodology, while capturing outcome data that validates or challenges that methodology, performs the Externalization and Combination functions at industry scale. The 2020 practitioner codification study is especially relevant: practitioners valued and used the codified knowledge, suggesting this is not inherently reductive even for relational, experiential domains.

### Category 4: The Faith-and-Technology Tension

#### Subcategory 4.1: Digital Reluctance in Churches

**Source:** Campbell & Jordan (Ecclesial Practices, 2023); Flourishing Congregations Institute; COVID Religion Research Project

- **DOK 1:** A study of 478 church leaders identified "digital reluctance" as an ideological factor beyond practical barriers, active pushback when digital solutions were suggested. Canadian Christian leaders rated AI importance at 4.42/7 (fourth lowest across all trends). Researchers characterized a "technology-adverse culture held by Christian leaders." During COVID, technology innovation was "the most stressful" new skill for religious leaders and "the one area for which they felt the most unprepared."
- **DOK 2:** Technology resistance in this space is not merely a usability or awareness problem, it has an ideological dimension rooted in theological identity. A platform must be designed with this in mind: it cannot lead with technology, data, or efficiency as primary value propositions. It must lead with the mission it serves (healthier church plants, more effective stewardship) and position technology as the invisible means, not the end.

#### Subcategory 4.2: The "Walk by Faith, Not by Sight" Objection

**Source:** Lifeway Research; FaithTech Institute; Christianity Today

- **DOK 1:** Many pastors view data analysis as "antithetical to what Scripture teaches," fearing churches become "filled with numbers, instead of the Spirit." The reconciliation framework proposed by practitioners and researchers: be "data-informed, faith-driven", "God's voice is always bigger than the data. With faith, God can do more than what we see in the data." Biblical counter-arguments include Numbers 1 and 26 (God commanding demographic databases), Luke 14 (Jesus inviting cost calculation), and Nehemiah (gathering community data to enable action). A 2026 Barna/Pushpay report indicates 33% of church leaders report using AI, and leaders are shifting from "whether" to "how."
- **DOK 2:** The framing of "data-informed, faith-driven" is not just a marketing message, it is a genuine theological position with scriptural support. A platform that adopts this framing authentically, presenting metrics as stewardship tools rather than performance scorecards, and explicitly preserving space for Spirit-led decisions that override what the data suggests, can navigate this tension. The fact that church leaders are already moving past "whether" to "how" suggests the window for a well-framed platform is opening.

#### Subcategory 4.3: Counter-Arguments to Standardized Methodology

**Source:** Lausanne Movement; Desiring God / Mack Stiles; MinTools; UMC Discipleship

- **DOK 1:** Critics argue that cookie-cutter approaches lessen effectiveness across different church cultures, backgrounds, communities, sizes, and contexts. The Lausanne Movement documents dramatic variation in church planting challenges by region. Mack Stiles identifies six dangers of church planting as a movement, including pragmatism over theological fidelity and over-contextualization. Assessment professionals acknowledge "there are no cookie-cutter answers" and "no magical assessments that automatically guarantee success." Research on franchise standardization shows an inverted U-shape, too little or too much standardization both reduce performance.

It must allow sending networks, denominations, and individual coaches to customize phases, milestones, content, and assessment criteria for their specific context. The "minimum critical specification" principle (Dees and Anderson, 2004) applies: codify only the fewest elements that produce impact. The platform provides structure, guardrails, not rails, and the methodology evolves based on outcome data rather than remaining static.

### Category 5: Platform Strategy and the Data Opportunity

#### Subcategory 5.1: Tool-First, Network-Later, The Canonical Strategy

**Source:** Chris Dixon (a16z, 2015); Eli Chait, "How the 100 Largest Marketplaces Solved the Chicken-and-Egg Problem" (2018); Choudary, Platform Thinking Labs; Eisenmann, Parker & Van Alstyne (HBR, 2006)

- **DOK 1:** Chris Dixon articulated the "come for the tool, stay for the network" strategy: a single-player tool provides immediate utility, attracting early adopters who get value without network effects; network features are introduced as the user base grows. Eli Chait's analysis of the 100 largest marketplaces found that 34% used single-player mode, achieving mean revenue of $7.7B on mean funding of $771M, a 10x capital efficiency ratio, compared to 1x for other strategies. OpenTable (reservation software for restaurants, then consumer marketplace), Square (card reader for merchants, then payment network), and Amazon (retailer, then third-party marketplace) are canonical examples.
- **DOK 2:** This strategy maps precisely to the church planting use case. The planter-facing tool (guided phases, CRM, financial tracking, meeting management) provides day-one standalone value. The sending network gets portfolio analytics as planters adopt. As the data aggregates across plants, the platform becomes valuable for benchmarking, early warning, and eventually planter-network matching. Each layer of value reinforces the others, creating the flywheel. The 10x capital efficiency finding is significant for a market that may not attract traditional venture capital, it suggests the business can grow efficiently on its own revenues.
- **Links:** [Chris Dixon (2015)](https://cdixon.org/2015/01/31/come-for-the-tool-stay-for-the-network) | [Eli Chait (2018)](https://blog.elichait.com/2018/04/09/how-the-100-largest-marketplaces-solve-the-chicken-and-egg-problem/) | [Eisenmann et al. (2006)](https://hbr.org/2006/10/strategies-for-two-sided-markets)

#### Subcategory 5.2: The Measurement Gap in Social Sector Organizations

**Source:** Fruchterman (Stanford Social Innovation Review, 2016); Bridgespan Group; McKinsey; Sopact

- **DOK 1:** 76% of nonprofit leaders say measuring impact is a top priority, yet only 29% feel they are effective at demonstrating outcomes. The "Indicator Trap" describes organizations that track activities (training hours, sessions delivered) with precision while outcome indicators remain absent, vague, or disconnected. McKinsey's research shows companies that focus on performance measurement are 4.2 times more likely to outperform peers. Bridgespan distinguishes "measuring to prove" (external accountability) from "measuring to learn" (internal improvement), arguing both are necessary.
- **DOK 2:** The church planting industry is squarely in the Indicator Trap, tracking attendance, baptisms, and giving (activity metrics) without measuring community transformation, leadership development, or financial sustainability trajectory (outcome metrics). A platform that moves the industry from activity tracking to outcome measurement does not just help individual plants, it creates the evidence base for the entire field to learn what actually drives healthy, sustainable churches. This is the "measuring to learn" function that Bridgespan identifies, and it requires the kind of structured, longitudinal data collection that only a platform can provide.
- **Links:** [Fruchterman (2016)](https://ssir.org/articles/entry/using_data_for_action_and_for_impact) | [Bridgespan - What Is Impact Measurement](https://www.bridgespan.org/insights/nonprofit-organizational-effectiveness/what-is-impact-measurement-and-how-can-organizations-use-it) | [Sopact - Social Impact Metrics](https://www.sopact.com/use-case/social-impact-metrics)

#### Subcategory 5.3: The Broader Market Context

**Source:** Lifeway Research; Christian Today; Straits Research

- **DOK 1:** In 2024, approximately 3,800 new Protestant churches launched while approximately 4,000 closed, a net loss of about 200. To maintain the current church-to-population ratio through 2050, the US needs a net gain of approximately 2,000 churches annually; to grow it, 8,000-11,000 new churches per year (2-3x current rate). Congregations founded since 2000 are the only group recording overall membership growth within the SBC. The church management software market is valued at $247M-$886M with no dominant player focused on the planting journey.
- **DOK 2:** The math is stark: the US needs more church plants, not fewer, and the plants it launches need to survive at higher rates. New churches outperform established ones in membership growth, which means the planting process is not just maintaining, it is the primary growth engine. A platform that improves planting success rates and accelerates the pipeline addresses a structural need that is growing, not shrinking. The existing ChMS market provides a ceiling estimate for adjacent revenue but also confirms that no one has yet built for the planting segment specifically.
